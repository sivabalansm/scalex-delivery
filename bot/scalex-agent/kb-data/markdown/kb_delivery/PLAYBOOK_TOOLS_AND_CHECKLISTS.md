## The Account Growth Strategic Mapper

[DUAL PERSPECTIVE]

**Purpose**: Map whitespace and growth vectors so account plans become an incubation engine, not a renewal tracker.

**Buyer Use Case**: Use it to detect “apex predator” behaviors: proactive incubation and cross-domain expansion that may raise lock-in risk.

**Vendor Use Case**: Use it to find messes to volunteer for, align to roadmaps, and multi-thread relationships across stakeholders.

**Extracted Content**:

This isn't a CRM; it's a "Whitespace" map. Fill this out for your Top 3 accounts:
1. The "Mess" Audit: Where is the client currently using temporary staff, interns, or high-cost internal resources for manual, undefined tasks?
2. Product Roadmap Alignment: What are the 3 biggest product launches or market expansions the client has planned for the next 18 months? Who owns the "Scaling" budget for those?
3. The Taxonomy Gap: Use the Taxonomy. In which Domains (Level 1) and Categories (Level 2) are we currently invisible?
4. Relationship Multi-Threading: List every VP in the target business unit. Do we have a "Warm Intro" to them? If not, who is our current champion that can make it happen?
Goal: Turn your account plan from a "Renewal Tracker" into an "Incubation Engine."

## The "Graceful Exit" Playbook

[DUAL PERSPECTIVE]

**Purpose**: Ensure a termination is handled professionally to protect continuity, reputation, and future deals.

**Buyer Use Case**: Use it to demand clean data return, refreshed SOPs, and a clean overlap plan that minimizes transition tax.

**Vendor Use Case**: Use it to exit like a class act: fast KT, radical honesty, and a dedicated exit manager KPI’d on a clean divorce.

**Extracted Content**:

A checklist to ensure you leave with your reputation (and your next deal) intact:
* [ ] The "Clean Data" Audit: Proactively return all client IP and data before they have to ask.
* [ ] The SOP Refresh: Update all Level 3 Process Maps one last time to reflect the current state.
* [ ] The Talent Transition Plan: Be transparent with your delivery team. Help them find new roles so they don't sabotage the service in their final 30 days.
* [ ] The Executive Farewell: Have your CEO call the client VP to personally thank them for the partnership and offer a smooth handoff.
Goal: Turn a termination into a "Pause" rather than a "Stop." Leave the door so far open that they can't help but walk back through it in two years.

## The Rate Bridge Calculator

[DUAL PERSPECTIVE]

**Purpose**: Explain cost-down proposals transparently by showing exactly what changes (volume, SLA, automation) drive the new price.

**Buyer Use Case**: Use it to validate that savings come from real changes (scope, service levels, efficiency) rather than random discounting.

**Vendor Use Case**: Use it to avoid the “you were overcharging me” reaction by showing the bridge from current spend to target spend.

**Extracted Content**:

This tool allows you to show the client exactly how you are getting to a lower number. It "bridges" the gap between the current state and the target state.
Columns in the Tool:
1. Current Monthly Spend: The baseline.
2. Volume Adjustments: Savings from removing "waste" or low-value tasks.
3. Service Level Trade-offs: Savings from moving from "Gold" to "Silver" SLAs.
4. Automation Impact: Savings from tech-led efficiency.
5. New Monthly Spend: The target number.
Pro Tip: Never show a lower price without showing the "Rate Bridge." If you just give a lower number, the buyer assumes you were overcharging them before. If you show the Bridge, they understand you are re-engineering the business to save them money.


**Source**: Chapter 8 - How to “Lower Your Rates” While Maintaining Your Profit Margins (A Vendor’s Guide to Financial Magic)

## The Relationship "At-Risk" Diagnostic

[DUAL PERSPECTIVE]

**Purpose**: Diagnose early warning signals that the client is drifting and a replacement RFP is forming.

**Buyer Use Case**: Use it as a mirror: if you’re seeing repeated asks, friction, attrition, and silence, the relationship is failing.

**Vendor Use Case**: Use it monthly to stop death-by-papercuts before it becomes termination-by-email.

**Extracted Content**:

This is a monthly internal health check for your account team. If you answer "Yes" to more than two of these, your client is already taking calls from your competitors.
1. Stakeholder Change: Has the primary client stakeholder (the one who signed the deal) changed or been promoted in the last 90 days?
2. The "Effort" Check: Has the client asked for the same report or data point more than twice in the last month?
3. Margin Warfare: Are we charging for more than 2 "Out-of-Scope" small requests per month?
4. Attrition: Has our "Core Delivery Team" seen more than 15% attrition this quarter?
5. Innovation Silence: When was the last time we brought an unprompted "Optimization Idea" to the client? (If >90 days, you are coasting).
Goal: Identify the "papercuts" before they lead to a hemorrhage.

**Source**: Chapter 11 - How to Lose Your Client (Hint, It’s Not Always Missing SLAs)

## The Relationship Health Audit

[DUAL PERSPECTIVE]

**Purpose**: Measure relationship health by customer effort (delight) rather than SLA compliance alone.

**Buyer Use Case**: Use it to evaluate whether your vendor reduces cognitive load and acts like an “easy button.”

**Vendor Use Case**: Use it to build habits that create peace of mind, no surprises, and rockstar support for the VM.

**Extracted Content**:

This is a monthly checklist for Account Managers to score themselves on "Delight" rather than "Compliance."
1. Rockstar Support: Did we provide a data point or slide this month specifically designed to make our VM look good to their boss?
2. Surprise Audit: Did we flag a potential "Yellow" issue at least 72 hours before it turned "Red"?
3. The "Easy" Test: On a scale of 1-10, how much work did the client have to do this month to get us to do our job? (Goal is <3).
4. Inward Innovation: Did we suggest one fix to the client's internal processes that would improve our service delivery?
Goal: Transform the relationship from a transaction into an alliance.

**Source**: Chapter 9 - How to Delight Your Client After You’ve Won Them (Hint, It’s Not Always Hitting SLAs)

## The "Trigger & Response" Log

[DUAL PERSPECTIVE]

**Purpose**: Clarify and operationalize this playbook concept into a repeatable workflow.

**Buyer Use Case**: Use it to make evaluation/management decisions easier and more defensible.

**Vendor Use Case**: Use it to execute the playbook consistently and reduce buyer effort.

**Extracted Content**:

To build your "Sales Superpower," use this log after every difficult client interaction or internal "fire."
1. What was the Triggering Event? (e.g., The client criticized our response time in a group meeting).
2. What was the Physical Feeling? (e.g., Tightness in the throat, heat in the face).
3. What was the "Internal Narrative"? (e.g., "They think we’re incompetent. I’m going to get fired. I need to prove they are wrong.")
4. The "Therapist" Reframe: (e.g., "The client is stressed about an upcoming audit. They aren't attacking me; they are venting their anxiety about the process.")
5. The "Strategic" Response: (e.g., "I hear the frustration. Let’s look at the logs together so I can understand the bottleneck and we can fix it for the audit team.")
Goal: Close the gap between the stimulus and the response. The wider that gap, the better the salesperson.

**Source**: Chapter 13 - The Salesperson's Secret Weapon: Why Your Therapist Might Be Your Best Career Coach
