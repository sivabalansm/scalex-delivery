# Key Outsourcing Concepts & Frameworks

## 3-Level Service Taxonomy (Domain → Category → Process)

[DUAL PERSPECTIVE]

**The Concept**: Buyers don’t buy “BPO.” They buy solutions to painful problems. The taxonomy maps services from the department that feels pain (Domain), to the purchasable market segment (Category), to the day-to-day workflow (Process).

**For Buyers**: Structure RFPs at Level 2 (Category) and evaluate capability at Level 3 (Process). Use taxonomy language to reduce ambiguity and enable apples-to-apples comparisons.

**For Vendors**: Market at Level 2, differentiate at Level 3, and stop sounding like a generic commodity. Speaking the buyer’s taxonomy creates instant credibility.

**Source**: Chapter 2 - How Clients Actually Buy Services (A Taxonomy Deep Dive)

## RFP Scoring Methodology (Proposal Score Tool)

[DUAL PERSPECTIVE]

**The Concept**: A weighted scoring model converts subjective preferences into a documented Proposal Score. It’s used for fairness, competitive tension, due diligence—and CYA.

**For Buyers**: Define criteria questions, assign weights, calibrate scorers, and force clarity. Require responses to follow your formatting so the scorer burden doesn’t corrupt outcomes.

**For Vendors**: Optimize for scorer ease: direct answers, tight structure, transparent pricing, and flawless orals execution with written follow-ups.

**Source**: Chapter 4 - How to Win RFPs (and the Secret Science of the Score Tool)

## Pricing Models (T&M vs Fixed vs Unit-Based vs Outcome)

[DUAL PERSPECTIVE]

**The Concept**: Commercial models shape incentives. T&M rewards slowness, fixed price is brittle, unit-based rewards efficiency with volume protections, and outcome-based ties pay to business results.

**For Buyers**: Negotiate on structure: prefer models that align incentives and provide transparency. Demand legible total cost and documented assumptions.

**For Vendors**: Use structure to protect margins and trust: unit-based for mature work, outcome-based where measurable, and never rely on opaque complexity.

**Source**: Chapter 5 - How to Negotiate a Great (and Fair) Contract

## BATNA & Negotiation Blueprinting

[DUAL PERSPECTIVE]

**The Concept**: Blueprinting maps interests vs positions and the full universe of deal levers. BATNA and a calculated walk-away point remove emotion and prevent desperate concessions.

**For Buyers**: Solve for interests, not demands. Trade across levers (governance, reporting, risk, term length) and build packages that meet stakeholder needs without breaking the deal.

**For Vendors**: Value levers, identify smart trades, bundle offers, and execute a strict ‘if X then Y’ protocol. Know your walk-away point before you enter the room.

**Source**: Chapter 6 - Negotiation Blueprinting™: The Art and Science of Deal Mapping

## Customer Effort Score (CES) and Cognitive Load

[DUAL PERSPECTIVE]

**The Concept**: CES predicts renewal better than CSAT/NPS in this playbook’s view. Delight is reducing buyer effort and cognitive load—being the ‘easy button,’ not just SLA-green.

**For Buyers**: Measure how hard you have to work to get the vendor to do the job. Low-effort vendors create peace of mind and reduce management tax.

**For Vendors**: Deliver proactive summaries, early warnings, and no-action-needed fixes. Make buyer success easy and you become hard to replace.

**Source**: Chapter 9 - How to Delight Your Client After You’ve Won Them (Hint, It’s Not Always Hitting SLAs)

## Five Pillars of Buyer Delight

[DUAL PERSPECTIVE]

**The Concept**: Delight comes from peace of mind, no surprises, making the VM look like a rockstar, being a partner in problem-solving, and proactive internal help.

**For Buyers**: Demand behaviors that reduce anxiety and admin work: early warnings, executive-ready artifacts, and crisis partnership.

**For Vendors**: Build repeatable routines that deliver these five outcomes; stop treating delight as ‘being nice’ and treat it as being low maintenance.

**Source**: Chapter 9 - How to Delight Your Client After You’ve Won Them (Hint, It’s Not Always Hitting SLAs)

## Legacy vs Growth Outsourcing (the billion-dollar fork)

[DUAL PERSPECTIVE]

**The Concept**: Legacy outsourcing is mature scope + cost savings + commodity pressure. Growth outsourcing is building what doesn’t exist yet; speed to market is king.

**For Buyers**: Know which game you’re playing. Growth models demand partners who can build processes, tools, and talent fast; legacy models demand efficiency and cost control.

**For Vendors**: Incubate in growth, then pivot to quality and cost as the maturity curve moves. Survive the squeeze via self-cannibalization before procurement forces it.

**Source**: Chapter 10 - Grow a Billion Dollar Client (The Voldemort Playbook)

## Relationship At-Risk Indicators (5-question diagnostic)

[DUAL PERSPECTIVE]

**The Concept**: Relationships fail by papercuts: stakeholder change, repeated asks, margin warfare, attrition, and innovation silence are early signals.

**For Buyers**: Watch effort creep and responsiveness decline. When you’re training their managers or getting nickel-and-dimed, the relationship is already red.

**For Vendors**: Run the diagnostic monthly. Fix friction before you’re being benchmarked, and keep bringing unprompted optimization ideas.

**Source**: Chapter 11 - How to Lose Your Client (Hint, It’s Not Always Missing SLAs)
