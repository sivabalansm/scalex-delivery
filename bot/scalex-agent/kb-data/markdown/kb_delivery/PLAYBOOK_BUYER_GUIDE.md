## Evaluating RFP Responses: Why RFPs Exist (and how to use that leverage)

[BUYER INSIGHT]

RFPs are not a reading hobby. They exist for “Fairness and Transparency,” “Competitive Tension,” “Due Diligence,” and—most importantly—“CYA (Cover Your Ass).” If you design the process to reduce internal ambiguity, you reduce downstream blame when the deal goes sideways.

**What It Means**: A “good” RFP process is one you can defend two years later. The scoring model is your evidence trail.

**What to Look For**:
- Vendors who follow the format “to the letter” (they understand the scorers’ burden).
- Pricing that is legible in <5 minutes (complexity destroys trust).
- Answers that show they’re solving your problem, not just listing “services.”

**What to Demand**:
- A scoring rubric with weighted criteria, clear 1–10 anchors, and named scorers.
- A single “apples-to-apples” cost view (normalization assumptions documented).
- An orals process where every question is captured and answered in writing post-session.

**Red Flags**:
- Boilerplate response (obvious find/replace; doesn’t use your language).
- Missed deadlines (signals delivery unreliability).
- Badmouthing competitors (signals insecurity).

## RFP Scoring Reality: The Proposal Score Tool and the ‘made them win’ problem

[BUYER INSIGHT]

The “industry secret” is that teams sometimes subtly “made” the vendor they wanted have the highest score. That’s exactly why you need explicit criteria questions, weights, and calibrated scorers—so preference is forced to justify itself.

**What It Means**: If the score is the decision, your job is to ensure the score reflects real risk and real value—not vibes.

**What to Look For**:
- A vendor that makes your scorers’ life easy (clear structure, direct answers, low fluff).
- Orals presenters who don’t talk over the solution architect (execution discipline).
- Honesty under pressure (no “three guys in a basement” proprietary-tech hand-waving).

**What to Demand**:
- A requirement that responses mirror your question numbering and formatting.
- A live demo / deep dive with actual practitioners (not only sales).
- Written post-orals follow-ups to every question asked, same day.

**Red Flags**:
- Vague “proprietary AI” claims with deflection when probed.
- Fluff that forces scorers to hunt for answers.
- Typos and sloppy decks on large proposals (“lack of care”).

**Source**: Chapter 4 - How to Win RFPs (and the Secret Science of the Score Tool)

## Contract Leverage Beyond Price: Non-commercial terms buyers really care about

[BUYER INSIGHT]

The contract is “your pre-nup, your marriage vows, and your shared household budget all rolled into one.” The strongest leverage often isn’t price—it’s governance, reporting, innovation structure, enforceable SLAs, and exit readiness.

**What It Means**: A fair contract sets both parties up for success. One-sided paper victories become operational disasters later.

**What to Look For**:
- A vendor who proactively proposes governance and escalation paths (not waiting to be asked).
- Reporting that goes beyond SLA green lights into trends and insight.
- Innovation commitments that aren’t marketing fluff (concrete mechanism).

**What to Demand**:
- Robust governance framework (weekly ops / monthly strategic / quarterly executive) with escalation.
- Clear reporting requirements and formats; real-time dashboard where possible.
- Annual mandatory productivity credits (typically 3–5%) as the enforceable innovation mechanism.
- Exit management and knowledge transfer obligations baked into the MSA.

**Red Flags**:
- Vague ‘innovation’ promises with no measurable mechanism.
- SLA language with no client dependency definitions (sets you up for excuses).
- A vendor that fights hard on small non-commercial terms (signals future friction).

**Source**: Chapter 5 - How to Negotiate a Great (and Fair) Contract

## Pricing Models: Negotiate on structure, not just price

[BUYER INSIGHT]

“Negotiate on structure, not just price.” Buyers hate models that hide cost or misalign incentives. The playbook calls T&M “a trap,” fixed-price “brittle,” unit-based “the favorite,” and outcome-based “the holy grail.”

**What It Means**: The commercial model is a control system. It shapes vendor behavior for the entire contract term.

**What to Look For**:
- Clear total cost story with assumptions and volume protections.
- A vendor who can explain tradeoffs: SLA cost, transition complexity, and margin reality.
- Willingness to discuss unit-based or outcome-based structures where the process is mature.

**What to Demand**:
- A pricing model that aligns incentives (unit-based for mature processes; outcome-based where measurable).
- Transparency: cost drivers, volume bands, and how changes flow into pricing.
- Explicit transition plan cost/effort rather than “flip a switch” fantasy.

**Red Flags**:
- Complex pricing you can’t understand in five minutes.
- Unsustainably low pricing (“too good to be true”).
- Vendor refuses to discuss how SLAs affect staffing and cost.

**Source**: Chapter 5 - How to Negotiate a Great (and Fair) Contract

## Managing Transitions: Expect surgery, not a Gantt chart

[BUYER INSIGHT]

Transition is “the first six months of living together while undergoing invasive surgery.” Smooth transitions are less about vendor hype and more about managing the client’s internal mess: access, dependencies, and resistance.

**What It Means**: Your biggest transition risk is client-side: missing credentials, bad documentation, and stakeholders who want the vendor to fail.

**What to Look For**:
- A vendor who anticipates “Project Traitors” and stakeholder resistance.
- Process-level mapping discipline (no “just shadow and you’ll figure it out”).
- A technical access log with named client owners and escalation.

**What to Demand**:
- A dedicated transition manager (not an AE or ops lead doing it as a side job).
- A dependency tracker that quantifies impact (“missed by 4 days → shifted by 11”).
- Vendor-owned documentation creation as part of KT (assume yours is garbage).

**Red Flags**:
- Vendor treats transition as checkbox project management.
- No plan for access management / IT ‘fortress’ realities.
- Transition owned by sales or steady-state lead instead of a specialist.

**Source**: Chapter 7 - How to Transition Like a Pro: The Things That “Can’t” Go Wrong (and How to Help Your Client Help You)

## What to Demand from Vendors: The ‘Easy Button’ and reducing your cognitive load

[BUYER INSIGHT]

The myth: “As long as the dashboard is green, the client is happy.” Reality: vendors get fired at 100% SLA compliance because they are a pain to manage. Delight is reducing your Customer Effort Score (CES) and cognitive load.

**What It Means**: A good vendor makes the work feel like it “runs itself.” Low maintenance beats slightly cheaper pricing in practice.

**What to Look For**:
- Reports delivered early with an executive summary you can copy-paste.
- Problems found and fixed proactively (“no action needed from you”).
- Respectful pushback instead of yes-manning impossible requests.

**What to Demand**:
- A ‘no surprises’ policy: early warnings with a mitigation plan (yellow is good).
- Governance where meetings start with what’s coming next month and how they’re getting ahead of it.
- Rockstar support artifacts (e.g., a QBR slide designed to make your VM look good).
- Crisis behavior: solve the fire first, then talk commercial impact.

**Red Flags**:
- Green dashboards but high effort: repeated follow-ups to get basic data.
- Bad news delivered late (Friday afternoon surprises).
- Vendor leads with change orders during crises.

**Source**: Chapter 9 - How to Delight Your Client After You’ve Won Them (Hint, It’s Not Always Hitting SLAs)

## Signs Your Vendor is Failing: High Effort, bait-and-switch, and the ‘silence of the dashboard’

[BUYER INSIGHT]

Vendor failure is usually “death by a thousand papercuts.” The hallmark is becoming a “High Effort” partner: more oversight, more reminders, more admin work. Watch for the “A-Team to C-Team” bait-and-switch and complacency masked by quiet status calls.

**What It Means**: If you’re spending hours training vendor managers on your business, you’re doing their job. That’s the moment the next RFP starts forming.

**What to Look For**:
- Retention of the rockstar team shown during the RFP/orals.
- Whether small requests are handled flexibly or turned into friction.
- Multi-threaded relationships (you’re not dependent on one person).
- Whether QBRs are value-driven or just ‘we hit the SLA.’

**What to Demand**:
- Key personnel / continuity commitments (and penalties or remedies).
- A minor-changes allowance or a policy to ‘gift the small stuff’ below a threshold.
- A relationship health metric (effort) in addition to SLA compliance.
- Multi-threading plan: exec sponsors + operational counterparts mapped.

**Red Flags**:
- Rockstars vanish post-go-live; junior team has no autonomy.
- Nickel-and-diming small requests (“SOW as a weapon”).
- Vendor fails to re-sell value after org changes / re-orgs.
- Silence + boring QBRs + no new ideas for 90+ days.

**Source**: Chapter 11 - How to Lose Your Client (Hint, It’s Not Always Missing SLAs)

## Managing Terminations: Demand a ‘class act’ exit (clean divorce) to reduce transition tax

[BUYER INSIGHT]

Terminations are brutal, but “the outsourcing world is remarkably tiny.” The question isn’t just why you’re exiting—it’s “How were they during the breakup?” A professional, fast exit reduces overlap costs and protects continuity.

**What It Means**: Your exit plan is a risk-control mechanism. It reduces vendor lock-in anxiety and prevents operational disruption.

**What to Look For**:
- Willingness to do a radical-honesty post-mortem.
- An exit manager who is not the grieving AE.
- Clean handoff artifacts: SOPs/process maps, data, and knowledge transfer.

**What to Demand**:
- Exit management clause specifying data return, documentation handoff, KT, and timelines.
- A clean overlap plan to minimize the 6-month transition window where you pay two vendors.
- Behavior standards: no data hostage tactics, no service-level tanking on the way out.

**Red Flags**:
- Vendor becomes defensive and disengaged immediately after notice.
- A-team pulled instantly; skeleton crew left behind.
- Any hint of holding documentation/data hostage (knowledge graveyard behavior).

**Source**: Chapter 12 - How to Survive Getting Terminated (An Outsourcing Marriage Story)
